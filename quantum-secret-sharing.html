Quantum Secret Sharing and Implications for Financial Infrastructure

Becky Ting · April 26, 2026

View PDF View Code Full paper · code repo includes the IBM Quantum Composer circuits

Abstract

Classical threshold secret-sharing schemes (Shamir, Blakley) provide an information-theoretic guarantee on the shares themselves, but the surrounding infrastructure — the channels used to distribute shares, and the classical cryptographic primitives (RSA, ECDSA, ECDH) used to authenticate participants — remains vulnerable to a quantum adversary via Shor's algorithm. This paper develops the case for Quantum Secret Sharing (QSS) as a physically grounded alternative, builds the Hillery– Bužek–Berthiaume (HBB) protocol from first principles using GHZ-state entanglement, verifies it experimentally on the IBM Quantum Composer statevector simulator, and evaluates its near-term application to interbank settlement infrastructure such as SWIFT.

Approach

The paper builds up from the classical secret-sharing problem and Shamir's polynomial-interpolation construction, through the structure of GHZ states and the GHZ–Mermin no-go argument establishing that their correlations cannot be reproduced classically, to a full construction and security proof of the HBB (2,3)-threshold protocol. The protocol was implemented as four measurement circuits on the IBM Quantum Composer statevector simulator (1,024 shots each), verifying logical correctness across all basis combinations with a mean reconstruction fidelity of 94.0%.

Key findings

Both single-party security (neither of two custodians can recover the secret alone) and eavesdropper security (interception during distribution is either uninformative or detectable, via the no-cloning theorem) are established formally and confirmed in simulation. The paper's outlook section maps this onto real financial infrastructure — SWIFT's existing multi-custodian HSM ceremonies already mirror the threshold structure QSS formalizes — while noting the open engineering problems: entanglement distribution range, decoherence and fault tolerance, and device-independent security.

Data & code

This paper is theory- and simulation-based rather than dataset-driven; see the linked GitHub repository for the four HBB circuit constructions run on the IBM Quantum Composer.
