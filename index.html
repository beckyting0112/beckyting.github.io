Your Name

I am a recent graduate of Yale School of Management, with a background in Mathematics and Computer Science and experience in investment and corporate strategy.

This is my personal website, where I document my research, writing, and some of the academic work that has shaped my thinking. Together, these projects reflect an evolving set of questions about how complex systems change — and how I came to be interested in the institutions, policies, and actors that shape that change.

Personal Journey

I have studied and worked across mathematics, computer science, business, investment, and corporate strategy. Each step gave me a new way to look at complex systems and made me curious about new questions.

Mathematics & Computer Science

Durham, UK

I began my studies in Mathematics and Computer Science at Durham University. I liked the structure of mathematical problems and the way computer science could turn abstract ideas into useful tools.

Over time, I became more interested in what these tools could help us understand. In a course on computational modelling for the humanities, I learned how to analyze large collections of data and find patterns using techniques such as topic modelling and sentiment analysis.

That experience changed my focus. I started thinking more about how technology affects the way businesses and societies work. This interest led me to business school.

Business School

HEC Paris & Yale School of Management

I decided to go to graduate school at HEC Paris and Yale School of Management because I wanted to understand how businesses work.

Living and studying in different countries exposed me to different economic systems and ways of thinking about business and society. My classes also took me beyond the traditional business curriculum. At Yale, I studied subjects including law, economic policy, and emerging technologies such as quantum computing.

I became more interested in how different fields connect: how new technologies create economic questions, how those questions become political, and how political choices in turn affect technology. I wanted to learn about these connections not just in the classroom, but also through real-world experience.

My Gap Year in Shanghai

China

During my year in Shanghai, I wanted to learn about China from two sides: its innovation sector and its traditional manufacturing industries. I did this through two internships that gave me very different perspectives on the same economy.

Investment

At Sinopharm-CICC Capital, I worked in private equity, evaluating companies and emerging industries in a fast-changing regulatory and technological environment.

The experience taught me that businesses are more than financial models. To understand an investment, I often had to understand the policy environment around it: how rules shape markets, how government priorities affect industries, and how new technologies create both opportunities and risks. I started to see that markets depend on the institutions around them.

Industrial Strategy

Later, I worked in corporate strategy at ArcelorMittal, one of the world's largest steel companies. This gave me a view of the global economy from the perspective of a multinational industrial company. I worked on issues including supply chains, trade policy, tariffs, and the changing structure of China's steel industry.

One project I found especially interesting was studying China's capacity replacement program and its transition toward electric arc furnaces. At first, I saw it as a quantitative problem and built models to understand how the industry was changing. But as I learned more, I saw that outcomes depended on more than market forces and central government policy. Local incentives, regional development goals, geography, and global supply chains also played important roles. (I later wrote an essay about this.)

This experience also showed me how analysis is used in practice. The same question may need to be explained very differently to a C-suite executive, an investor, or an operations team.

At this point, my interests began to focus more explicitly on political economy.

Where I Am Going

I realized that the work I find most rewarding is research: starting with a question, working through incomplete or sometimes contradictory information, and trying to understand the forces shaping an outcome.

I am drawn to questions at the intersection of technology, economics, and political systems. In the short term, I am seeking opportunities in research and corporate strategy, where I can continue exploring complex questions and turn analysis into decisions.

In the long term, I hope to pursue a PhD in International Political Economy. I want to study how states and institutions respond to technological and economic change, especially when the effects extend beyond national borders.
