Mapping Polarization in U.S. Party Platforms from 2008–2024 with Topic Modeling and Sentiment Analysis

Yeuk Wai Ting · December 14, 2025

View PDF View Code Full paper with figures · code repo includes the BERTopic/sentiment pipeline

Abstract

This study examines the evolution of thematic structure and rhetorical tone in U.S. party platforms from 2008 to 2024, using a three-stage NLP pipeline — document embeddings, topic modeling, and sentiment analysis — applied to Manifesto Project quasi-sentence data. Democrats and Republicans increasingly emphasize distinct clusters of issues and frame shared domains, such as healthcare, immigration, and technology, in different ways, and the emotional tone associated with these topics has become more polarized. The findings suggest that recent polarization reflects not just ideological divergence, but a growing separation in how the parties define political problems, construct governing agendas, and communicate them to the public.

Method

The corpus draws on nine party manifestos (five Democratic, four Republican) from the Manifesto Project Database, processed at the quasi-sentence level. Sentence-BERT embeddings feed into BERTopic (UMAP dimensionality reduction + HDBSCAN clustering) to recover 35 broad topics per party, with a second layer of subtopic modeling within healthcare, immigration, and technology. A RoBERTa-large sentiment classifier (siebert/sentiment-roberta-large-english) scores emotional tone within each topic-year cell.

Key findings

Republican platforms show far greater document-level continuity across cycles (cosine similarity 0.78–0.81) than Democratic ones, which fluctuate more sharply around major shocks like the ACA reorganization and COVID-19. Within shared topics, the parties diverge sharply in both internal composition and sentiment: Democratic healthcare and technology framing stays consistently positive, while Republican immigration framing is consistently and increasingly negative from 2016 onward — evidence that polarization operates at the level of agenda construction, not just policy preference.
