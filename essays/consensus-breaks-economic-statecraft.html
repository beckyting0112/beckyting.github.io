When Consensus Breaks: The Improvised Turn in U.S. Economic Statecraft

Your Name

How should we think about US international economic policy in the context of America's overall domestic and foreign policy? How dramatic are the changes now taking place and where might they lead?

U.S. international economic policy has never been merely technical. It reflects domestic political sentiments and America's broader vision of its role in the international system. The past decades of globalization endured not only because of ideological faith in free markets but because economic openness reinforced domestic stability while extending U.S. influence abroad. When this alignment between domestic and foreign interests held, American leadership appeared natural and legitimate. The recent shift toward tariffs and export controls reflects a broader retrenchment in foreign policy, oscillating back toward withdrawal in a cycle observed by Henry Kissinger. As he wrote in Diplomacy, U.S. foreign policy has oscillated between international engagement and withdrawal.1 This essay argues that the recent development is as much an adaptive response to unresolved domestic conditions as a return to protectionism.

I. The Ideology Shift

The post-WWII period acts as an appropriate benchmark for understanding contemporary U.S. economic policy. The postwar era marks the consolidation of U.S. hegemony, when the U.S. replaced Britain as the leader of the global economic system. Subsequent transformations in foreign economic policy build upon this foundation. Comparing the current reorientation with the postwar order juxtaposes the beginning of American hegemony alongside what may be an early stage of its decline.

Charles Maier describes the core of the postwar order as the politics of productivity.2 Policymakers prioritized productivity growth to avoid the political cost of addressing class conflicts and wealth distribution. Rising output allowed wages, profits, and state revenues to increase simultaneously, defusing distributional tensions without fundamentally altering power relations. Growth was thus both an economic objective and a political strategy.2

International openness was central to this arrangement. As John Ruggie's concept of embedded liberalism emphasizes, trade and financial integration were structured to support domestic social stability.3 Expanding markets abroad stabilized allied economies, reinforced capitalist legitimacy, and generated political support for openness at home. Economic expansion served a dual purpose: it underwrote domestic welfare while consolidating American leadership abroad.

With the collapse of the Soviet Union, U.S. policymakers increasingly linked the free market to political moderation. Elite consensus emerged around the idea that integrating states into the global economy would socialize them into a liberal order. Decisions such as the 1994 decoupling of China's Most Favored Nation trade status from its human rights record illustrate this faith in economic integration over political conditions.

Yet the historical alignment that underpinned these policies was fragile. The postwar settlement depended largely on sustained productivity growth. As globalization deepened, expansion could no longer substitute for redistribution. Gains became uneven, domestic legitimacy eroded, and the social foundations of global trade weakened. It is this erosion, along with the rise of China, that explains the turn toward a security-oriented economic strategy today. The new economic statecraft, which focuses on financial leverage and selective access from even allies, can be understood as a best response to the breakdown of the domestic foundations that once made expansive liberalism politically viable. In other words, the postwar settlement serves as both a mirror and a caution: where domestic legitimacy aligned with international openness, policy could expand; where it eroded, economic tools are repurposed for control and protection. This shift parallels the cycle of an empire: from establishing and expanding to defending it.

II. Domestic Policies

Because the postwar settlement relied on productivity growth to reconcile competing domestic interests without directly resolving class conflicts, the underlying tensions between winners and losers never disappeared. They only re-emerged as globalization deepened, weakening the case for U.S. global leadership at home.

The so-called "China shock" accelerated these dynamics. Rapid trade integration with China exposed stark domestic inequalities: manufacturing employment declined sharply in specific regions, particularly the American Midwest, while policy responses remained limited. China became a focal point for grievances over deindustrialization, wage stagnation, and perceived elite indifference. It was not the sole cause of backlash but a lens through which broader domestic pressures were externalized.

By the early 2000s, the gap between elite consensus and public opinion widened. Optimism about globalization in the 1990s had allowed elites to pursue engagement with limited pushback. By 2024, only 56 percent of Americans supported an active U.S. role in world affairs, a near-historic low that highlights the fragility of the domestic foundations of international leadership.5 The lack of public support meant that globalization became politically and fiscally constrained.

More crucially, there is a lack of a bipartisan "center." While there is now a rare bipartisan agreement on the existence of strategic threats, particularly regarding China and technological competition, there is a profound lack of consensus on the methods required to counteract them. Because the U.S. is so polarized, both parties increasingly use economic tools for immediate political gain rather than long-term strategic stability. This shift is evident in the partisan flip on engagement: while Republicans were once more favorable to a global role, by 2024, only 54 percent of Republicans supported active engagement compared to 68 percent of Democrats.5

The resulting foreign economic policies are often ad hoc and performative. Without a stable bipartisan "center," economic statecraft becomes an instrument for signaling domestic resolve while sidestepping the difficult internal task of rebuilding the social contract. By securitizing trade, investment, and technology, policymakers could discipline domestic and international rivals and respond to some domestic demands for economic protection. Consequently, the state acts decisively in international arenas not out of ideological strength but as a pragmatic response to domestic political erosion.

III. Effect on Foreign Policy and its Toolkits

As the domestic foundations for globalization weakened, U.S. international economic policy was repurposed toward national security. The state now prioritizes leverage, conditionality, and selective control over broad openness. At the center of this toolkit lies New Economic Statecraft proposed by Aggarwal & Reddie: the strategic use of trade, finance, technology, and investment controls to shape the behavior of other states by exploiting asymmetries in global economic networks.4 For instance, the U.S. maintains a dominant 80% share in global financial service provision, which provides the leverage needed to use sanctions like exclusion from the SWIFT system.6

Specific chokepoints are targeted now, where U.S. dominance is difficult to replicate. A prime example of this "relational power" is seen with the Dutch firm ASML; because ASML relies on U.S. intellectual property for its lithography machines, the U.S. can effectively force the company to stop selling to China.6 This deepens the embedding of firms in the execution of foreign policy, as corporations become the intermediaries through which economic power is exercised. Corporate incentives, lobbying (most notable from tech giants), and risk assessments now influence the scope, credibility, and enforceability of U.S. economic coercion more than before. However, unlike the use of military force, which is governed by strict, long-established rules of engagement and escalation, U.S. economic statecraft currently lacks a guiding doctrine or institutional safeguards to prevent abuses.7

IV. How Dramatic Is the Shift?

The current reorientation of U.S. international economic policy is dramatic in form, but not entirely unprecedented in logic. Transactionalism had been a feature of U.S. statecraft before. During World War II, the United States imposed stringent terms on Britain through cash-and-carry arrangements and the 1940 destroyers-for-bases deal, even as Britain faced existential threats.

What is new is the institutional and doctrinal context. Earlier episodes of transactional pressure were embedded in broader schemes to build an American economic order. Coercive leverage applied to Britain ultimately gave way to Lend-Lease and to the institutionalization of U.S. leadership through Bretton Woods and postwar alliances.

In contrast, today's economic strategy operates without a clearly articulated blueprint.

While a Realist perspective suggests that any hegemon would naturally weaponize technological chokepoints and secure its "near abroad" against a peer competitor like China, the current implementation of this strategy risks a self-defeating "overreach" that undermines the very power it seeks to preserve. The transactional character of current economic diplomacy increasingly targets traditional allies with the same severity as strategic rivals. Tariff rates, exemptions, and coordination are now negotiated case-by-case with this Administration. By replacing norm-driven alignment with conditional, interest-driven cooperation, the U.S. risks transforming from a "security provider" that anchors the global system into a mere "security enabler" that operates through coercion. This indiscriminate use of structural power prompts allies to hedge by constructing parallel financial and technological systems to dilute U.S. dominance. Consequently, these policies may dismantle the crucial alliances that have historically served as the ultimate force multipliers of American power in the face of rising rivalry.

The lack of a coherent blueprint for the direction and strategic value of U.S. foreign economic policy is most evident in the 2025 National Security Strategy (NSS), which elevated economics to the "ultimate stakes" of national power.8 This doctrine facilitates an isolationist retrenchment by prioritizing the Western Hemisphere as America's highest strategic priority — effectively relegating traditional geopolitics in the Indo-Pacific and Middle East to a supporting role. Central to this "Donroe" doctrine is a fundamental misdiagnosis of the threat: it defines China primarily as an economic competitor to be managed through rebalanced ties, rather than a multidimensional strategic rival whose ambitions extend far beyond trade.8 This lack of strategic foresight and institutional discipline carries significant risk. By retreating into a resource-centric sphere of influence and favoring an "alliance of illiberals" based on nationalist conservative values rather than democratic norms, the U.S. risks ceding the diplomatic and strategic ground necessary to lead a global consensus. Without a clear doctrine to guide its economic weapons, the U.S. remains an engine of control in a shrinking sphere, vulnerable to the very fragmentation its policies have accelerated.

Historically, U.S. grand strategy has been sustainable only when domestic political arrangements and international economic commitments were mutually reinforcing. Viewed in this light, the current trajectory is not a settled grand strategy but a transitional response to unresolved domestic constraints. While recent shifts in economic statecraft are abrupt and highly visible, they lack the coherence and institutional grounding that characterized earlier periods of sustainable leadership. It is unlikely that the present configuration will remain in place. Whether through electoral turnover, fiscal pressure, or the cumulative effects of allied and market responses, some recalibration of domestic policy will be required if the United States is to restore a workable grand strategy. This necessity for recalibration presents an opportunity for strategists to "zero out" inherited premises and conduct a "zero-based" review of foreign policy, creating a clean slate from which to include public interest and rebuild the nation's long-held interests and values for a new era.10

Conclusion

The recent reorientation of U.S. international economic policy reflects more than strategic rivalry; it is a structural symptom of a fractured internal consensus. The previous settlement, which relied on productivity growth to bridge partisan divides and sustain global leadership, has reached its limits. Moving forward requires a fundamental shift in domestic priorities to reconcile these competing interests and align foreign policy with broad public interests. This period of transition offers a rare opportunity for a fresh start. By reconstructing a grand strategy from the ground up, one that integrates the needs of the citizenry with strategic objectives, the United States can forge a more sustainable and coherent international role. Without such an internal realignment, economic policy will likely remain reactive and transactional — an engine of control rather than a stable foundation for cooperation.
