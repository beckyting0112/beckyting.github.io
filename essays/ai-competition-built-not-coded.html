The Next Phase of AI Competition Will Be Built, Not Just Coded

Your Name

The landscape of AI competition has shifted in recent years. While early progress was driven by algorithmic breakthroughs and the ability to scale software rapidly, the current phase is defined by the integration of AI into physical infrastructure, industrial production, logistics, and military systems. In the initial stage, the United States was able to leverage its strengths in research, capital, and software ecosystems. Yet as the sources of competitive advantage are changing, it is no longer sufficient to focus on model alone; the real challenge lies in coordinating infrastructure, industry, and alliances to support and deploy these technologies effectively.

I. The AI Value Chain: From Frontier Innovation to Systems Competition

Upstream:

The United States currently leads in AI accelerator design, with companies like NVIDIA at the forefront. However, this leadership is sustained through a complex network of alliances. For instance, semiconductor fabrication relies on TSMC, while advanced lithography is dependent on ASML. Japan and South Korea provide essential materials and memory components. Maintaining competitiveness in these upstream domains, therefore, requires ongoing coordination and collaboration with allies.

Such interdependence can be a source of strength, but it is also inherently fragile. Middle powers have become increasingly conscious of the leverage they hold through control of key technologies. The Netherlands, for example, can adjust export controls on lithography equipment, while South Korea navigates its semiconductor partnerships between the United States and China. These countries are unlikely to sacrifice their own economic interests for the sake of great power rivalry. Over time, as technological competition endures, they may choose to hedge their bets, diversifying their economic relationships for flexibility.

China's upstream strengths are different in character. In energy and critical minerals, Beijing is more self-sufficient and systemically coordinated. AI scaling is electricity-intensive, with data center demand now projected to reach nearly 10% of total U.S. electricity consumption. Beijing treats electrification as a component of national power, integrating grid expansion and solar production into a unified strategy. For example, it has constructed ultra-high-voltage transmission networks connecting energy-rich western provinces to coastal demand centers. Through initiatives such as "East Data, West Compute," Chinese authorities have directed data processing toward regions with abundant power while coordinating industrial siting at a national scale.

Although the United States possesses significant energy resources, efforts to modernize the power grid are often hampered by political fragmentation. Large technology firms, such as Microsoft, have managed to secure private energy sources — for example, through agreements like the Three Mile Island nuclear restart — but these initiatives cannot address the broader challenges of federal permitting and regulatory reform. If China is able to coordinate infrastructure development more efficiently, it could potentially counterbalance the advantages the United States holds at the level of AI models. In this context, energy security and AI competitiveness have become closely intertwined.

The situation regarding human capital is more complex. The United States continues to attract leading AI researchers, yet increasing barriers to immigration have begun to undermine this advantage. While the O-1 visa remains an important route for some, the limitations of the H-1B system have prompted many experts to seek opportunities elsewhere.

China, in contrast, is investing in domestic AI education and welcoming talent that might otherwise have remained in the U.S. Ultimately, the competition for talent will depend less on ideology and more on the predictability and stability offered by each country. Unless the United States takes deliberate steps to streamline high-skilled immigration, its edge in attracting global researchers may erode gradually over time.

Downstream:

The most dynamic shifts in competitive balance are now occurring in the downstream phase, where AI is deployed at scale in industry. China, for example, leads the world in the installation of industrial robots and has integrated AI across sectors such as electric vehicles, battery manufacturing, and advanced machinery. This scale is not simply a matter of adoption; it creates feedback loops that drive further technological refinement. Predictive maintenance systems, for instance, improve through continuous data collection, while robotics integration enhances coordination between control systems and AI. These cycles not only boost domestic productivity but also contribute to the ongoing improvement of the underlying AI models.

The scale of downstream deployment also has important geopolitical consequences. China has shown a capacity to bundle telecommunications infrastructure, construction expertise, state-backed financing, and digital platforms into comprehensive export packages. When AI-enabled industrial systems, such as smart factories and grid management software, are included in these packages, recipient countries may find themselves adopting not only Chinese hardware but also Chinese technical standards and data architectures. Adoption, in this case, is often driven by convenience and cost-effectiveness, rather than by technological superiority alone. By contrast, the United States has traditionally excelled in exporting high-end software, semiconductors, and services, but has been less consistent in offering integrated packages that combine infrastructure, industrial systems, and platforms.

None of this implies an inevitable Chinese advantage. China's state-directed model carries structural weaknesses. For example, their data governance constraint may limit international adoption. The U.S. remains unmatched in semiconductors, deep integration into global academic systems, and financial architectures capable of scaling frontier firms rapidly.

It is becoming clear that leadership in model development alone may not be enough. Long-term influence will likely depend on the ability to achieve systemic integration across multiple domains, which requires cooperation between the government and private sector.

II. Political Risk and the Fragility of Integration

The main risk to coordination right now is the lack of structure in its implementation. The recent Pentagon's threats to invoke the DPA against Anthropic illustrate how security imperatives can become coercive, risking public trust and innovation incentives. Meanwhile, frontier AI firms invest heavily in political influence: OpenAI co-founder Greg Brockman, Palantir co-founder Joe Lonsdale, and Andreessen Horowitz–backed PACs have funneled over $125 million into state-level campaigns to shape AI governance, often framing regulation as a competitive threat to U.S. AI leadership. This is not inherently problematic; major industries have long participated in policymaking. The risk arises when regulatory frameworks emerge through narrow, firm-specific negotiations rather than through broad institutional design. Fragmented rules tailored to particular actors can produce uneven standards, reduce interoperability, and create perceptions of favoritism. Over time, this undermines both public legitimacy and alliance confidence.

III. Institutionalization as Strategic Architecture

Institutionalization, in this context, does not mean adopting a centrally planned approach like China. Rather, it is about reducing uncertainty and aligning incentives within a decentralized system of innovation.

1. Codified AI Doctrine and Use Frameworks

As AI systems migrate from research environments into defense logistics, intelligence analysis, and industrial automation, ambiguity about acceptable use becomes strategically costly. Firms require clarity regarding liability, oversight expectations, and escalation thresholds, particularly in dual-use or military contexts.

A codified national AI doctrine would establish baseline principles for deployment in defense and critical infrastructure. These could include explicit human-in-the-loop requirements for lethal applications, audit standards for safety-critical systems, and clear delineation between training data access and operational control. Such doctrine would reduce reliance on executive improvisation and minimize firm-by-firm negotiation over politically sensitive use cases.

A national AI infrastructure coordination mechanism — potentially housed within an interagency body linking the Department of Energy, Department of Commerce, and Department of Defense — could serve as a planning interface rather than a command authority. Its function would be to identify compute-intensive clusters, align grid modernization priorities, accelerate permitting for critical facilities, and coordinate with state governments.

Crucially, such a mechanism would not seek to control research and development at the firm level or prescribe how models are designed. Instead, its purpose would be to reduce barriers in infrastructure, ensuring that the energy and permitting systems can keep pace with the needs of a dynamic capital market. In this way, the United States can maintain its comparative advantage in innovation while addressing coordination challenges in areas like energy and land use.

2. Secure Public–Private Compute Environments

As frontier models increasingly intersect with national security and industrial systems, secure compute environments become necessary for joint development without compromising proprietary assets or alliance trust. A structured national AI research and deployment environment — building on proposals such as a National AI Research Resource — could provide:

Air-gapped or tiered secure compute for classified or sensitive applications, shared evaluation infrastructure for safety and robustness testing, and interoperability standards that reduce fragmentation across firms.

These environments would enable the state to access advanced capabilities without relying on emergency measures or coercive tactics. At the same time, firms would have reasons to participate, as they could benefit from the government's ability to defend against cyber threats and protect intellectual property.

3. Bipartisan Legislation

One of the most critical aspects of institutionalization is ensuring that policies endure across different administrations. When export controls, AI safety regulations, or industrial coordination mechanisms change abruptly with each election cycle, it can undermine the trust of allies and disrupt the planning of firms.

By embedding key AI governance frameworks in bipartisan legislation, the United States can reduce policy volatility and signal that its approach to integration is strategic, not merely political. This is especially important for middle powers, whose cooperation is essential for maintaining leverage in areas like semiconductors and critical minerals. Predictable policies reinforce the strength of alliances, while unpredictability can weaken them.

Institutionalization, then, should not be confused with heavy-handed government intervention. Rather, it involves building durable frameworks that support a decentralized system of innovation. The goal is to preserve the United States' core strengths — such as openness, deep capital markets, and strong alliances — while addressing coordination challenges in areas like energy, deployment, and export strategy.

IV. Implications for Geopolitics

Middle powers are pivotal nodes in the AI value chain and they face three potential strategic responses to great power rivalry: align with a major power, hedge, or pursue sovereignty.

The United States should aim to encourage alignment as much as possible. Achieving this goal depends on providing predictable, stable, and credible engagement, which can reinforce these alliances and reduce the appeal of alternative strategies.

China has shown that it can combine infrastructure financing, telecommunications, and digital platforms into ready-made export packages. Often, countries adopt Chinese technical standards because of cost or convenience, rather than because they are technologically superior. This underscores the importance of coordinated efforts among U.S. allies to guide middle powers toward American frameworks.

The biggest barrier to alliance is perhaps the loss of trust in U.S. leadership. A good starting point to rebuild that trust is working on a common issue, like critical minerals. The U.S. should lead and promise not to weaponize cooperation in these areas for unrelated issues.

V. Quantum Readiness and Strategic Urgency

Integrating AI across all layers — from upstream research to downstream deployment — is not only necessary today, but also a preparation for the future, the disruptive impact of quantum technologies. Advances in quantum computing and cryptography could transform sectors such as finance, energy, communications, and defense. However, these breakthroughs will only have strategic value if they are supported by a coherent and resilient ecosystem.

If integration across the entire technology stack is lacking, advances in quantum technologies could create new vulnerabilities rather than strategic advantages. Building scalable and reliable quantum systems depends on high-performance semiconductors, specialized manufacturing, sufficient energy resources, skilled personnel, and secure computing environments. When any of these elements are not well coordinated, the overall value of quantum breakthroughs is diminished. In the same way, effective deployment of quantum technologies among allies will require predictable frameworks and shared standards — much like those being developed for AI integration today.

The United States' longstanding strengths in research, capital markets, and alliances offer a strong foundation for preparing for quantum technologies. However, the move toward integrated systems in AI highlights a broader point: competitive advantage in new technologies is no longer determined only by innovation at the frontier. Instead, it depends on the ability of countries and their partners to align resources, industrial deployment, and infrastructure over the long term. In this sense, efforts to integrate AI today serve as a strategic investment in the operational and institutional capacity that will be needed when quantum technologies become viable.

Preparing for the impact of quantum technologies is less about forecasting the timing of specific breakthroughs and more about building a robust and interoperable ecosystem. By viewing comprehensive AI integration as an investment in strategic infrastructure, the United States and its allies can increase the likelihood that quantum advances will strengthen security and economic influence, rather than introducing new risks.

Conclusion

Competitive advantage in emerging technologies is no longer defined solely by the ability to achieve breakthrough innovations. Instead, it belongs to those countries and coalitions that can align resources, industrial deployment, infrastructure, and alliances over the long term. The need for a coherent national strategy has never been more pressing.
