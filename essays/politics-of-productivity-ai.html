The Politics of Productivity in the Age of AI

Your Name

"The strong do what they can, and the weak suffer what they must."

When Canadian Prime Minister Mark Carney quoted Thucydides to describe the geopolitics of 2026, he captured more than an international phenomenon. The same sentiment has been felt within the United States itself: between the federal government and the states, between urban and rural communities, and between those seen and those forgotten. This imbalance of power intensified polarization in America to a new level this year.

I. From Industrial to Service Economies

Productivity is not just an economic concept. It defines which forms of work are socially valued, protected, and stabilized. The "politics of productivity," as Charles S. Maier described in postwar America, deferred distribution conflicts with universal growth, efficiency, and increasing output.1 Under American industrial dominance, this framework consolidated both domestic legitimacy and international leadership. So long as productivity gains translated into rising wages and stable employment, the industrial middle class could serve as the social anchor, enabling the state to govern as a technocratic manager of growth rather than an explicit arbiter of class conflict.

By the late 1960s and 1970s, this settlement began to erode. The U.S. began its transition from a manufacturing economy to a service- and knowledge-based economy. According to BLS, in the 1950s, nonfarm manufacturing accounted for one-third of the workforce; by 2025, it had plummeted to approximately 8%.2 Some of the former industrial workers experienced downward mobility and status loss, while their economic displacement was largely reframed as individual failure rather than structural transformation. The loss of 5.7 million manufacturing jobs between 2001 and 2010 fueled the discontent that eventually sparked the Tea Party and the 2016 election of Donald Trump.3 The result was not merely economic dislocation, but political alienation: a "bittered" former middle class who felt left by society to absorb adjustment costs privately. The seeds of polarization were sown not solely by cultural change but by the neglect of a displaced middle class during a foundational productivity transition.

II. The Problem of Redistribution

Other Western countries have experienced the same economic transitions, which hints at a deeper driver of polarization in the U.S. The answer likely lies in the divergent ideologies regarding economic redistribution and social mobility.

A well-known but empirically supported distinction is how Americans and Europeans perceive the fairness of the market system. Americans tend to see markets as broadly fair and believe in the "American Dream" — that individual effort can move someone from "rags to riches." Wealth is therefore interpreted as a reward for talent and effort, while poverty is often framed as a failure to seize opportunity. Europeans, by contrast, are more inclined to view the economic system as structurally unfair, attributing wealth to family background and poverty to insufficient social protection.

Consistent with this, a study in intergenerational mobility (Alesina et al. 2018) found that Americans are systematically over-optimistic about intergenerational mobility.4 They significantly overestimate the probability that a child born into the bottom income quintile will reach the top quintile. Europeans display the opposite bias: they underestimate actual mobility, particularly the likelihood of escaping the bottom quintile. These beliefs map onto policy preferences, and crucially, by policy type. Individuals who perceive low social mobility tend to favor "equality of opportunity" interventions — such as public education and healthcare — far more than "equality of outcome" policies, such as progressive taxation or redistributive transfers.

The authors also identify a "responsiveness gap" across political ideologies. When presented with evidence of low mobility, Left-leaning respondents increase their support for redistributive policies. Right-leaning respondents, by contrast, show little change. One theory for this is that they interpret the same information as evidence of government failure or overreach, reinforcing their view that the state is the problem rather than the solution.4

In the U.S., these dynamics are further complicated by geography. Actual mobility is higher in the North and Northwest and lower in the South and Southeast, yet over-optimism is most pronounced precisely where mobility is lowest.4 One explanation is that high income segregation limits individuals' ability to observe upward mobility directly, so narratives of the American Dream persist even when local realities contradict them.

A further paradox emerges along racial and ethnic lines. African Americans and children of immigrants often exhibit higher optimism about mobility than other groups. This may reflect "system justification" — a psychological mechanism that reduces cognitive dissonance — or the tendency to evaluate progress relative to one's own group rather than the national average.4 Demographic diversity also interacts with redistribution preferences. Empirical evidence shows that greater local exposure to immigrants and minorities is associated with reduced support for redistribution, particularly among right-leaning respondents who may believe that redistributive benefits will accrue to an out-group rather than themselves.4

Taken together, these patterns point to a deeply rooted fragmentation of the middle class. As productivity gains and losses diverge, distributional conflict is increasingly refracted through cultural, geographic, and partisan lenses. Polarization intensifies because the political system lacks a shared framework for determining who deserves protection as the economy evolves.

III. Institutional Failure and the 2016 Critical Election

U.S. politics has evolved in ways that locked in polarization. The 2016 Presidential election marked a critical turning point in this process. From the perspective of critical election theory, 2016 constituted a realignment, but one that differs sharply from earlier critical elections such as 1968 (Nixon) and 1932 (Roosevelt). Earlier realignments produced new governing coalitions; the post-2016 realignment consolidated polarization as a governing strategy.

As Doug Sosnik argues, American politics has become nationalized: partisan identity now structures voting behavior across all levels of government. 95 out of 100 senators now belong to the same party as the presidential candidate who carried their state, a massive shift from the 1980s when over half the Senate was split-ticket.5 Geographic sorting has rendered most districts noncompetitive in general elections. This institutional configuration creates what can be described as a "Primary Trap." Politicians are more likely to lose primaries than general elections, incentivizing ideological purity, symbolic confrontation, and performative politics over compromise. This is structurally locked in by the collapse of competitive geography; of the 435 congressional districts, only 46 are currently considered competitive by the Cook Report, and of those, only three are in rural areas. For the vast majority of representatives, the only electoral threat that matters is from candidates within their own party.5

On the federal level, leadership increasingly disregarded legal and constitutional norms, not as an aberration but as a political asset. This is exemplified by the pardoning of over 1,500 felons related to the January 6 violence, a move that critics argue is intensifying mutual loathing.6 Further institutional strain appears in debates over the role of federal agencies in states and the protection of constitutional rights, such as the Second Amendment, sparked by the recent shooting of Alex Pretti.

In this polarized environment, economic policies are naturally not tackling social issues. Polarization becomes self-reinforcing, and international economic policy increasingly serves as a substitute arena for unresolved domestic conflict.

IV. AI and the Next Middle-Class Shock

Artificial intelligence represents the next, and potentially most destabilizing, redefinition of productivity. Unlike previous technological transitions, which unfolded over decades, AI and related technologies threaten to restructure production at unprecedented speed. Major tech firms like Google, Meta, and Microsoft have invested $360 billion in AI and data centers in a single year, accelerating a pace of investment that is already "hectic."3 This transition places the contemporary service-sector middle class at particular risk.

As William Galston warns, the political danger lies not in mass unemployment alone, but in widespread underemployment and status erosion among educated workers.3 Firms increasingly anticipate productivity gains without proportional employment growth. Amazon recently cut 14,000 corporate jobs, which is 10% of its white-collar workforce. Companies like UPS, Salesforce, and Microsoft have moved in the same direction. Some firms report even more drastic shifts; the CEO of SBI replaced 80% of his software development team with AI, and Chegg cut 45% of its workforce as AI began answering student questions.

For a middle class socialized into meritocratic narratives of productivity, the prospect that education and skill no longer guarantee security represents a profound shock. The class of 2025 graduates submitted more applications than the previous year but received fewer offers. It has not improved for the class of 2026.

The political economy in the U.S. is poorly prepared to manage this transition. The current middle class remains fragmented by ideology, geography, and occupation, and lacks collective institutions capable of translating economic risk into political demands, as argued in the previous three sections. This has fueled the "Rise of America's Hard Left," with 62% of young people now holding a favorable view of socialism as they face AI-related job destruction.8 This shift reflects not only material anxiety, but a perception that existing policies once again neglect middle-class adjustment.

We risk repeating earlier failures to align productivity gains with broader security. This is not to argue for expansive state intervention, but rather for effective oversight, regulation, and social safety nets to manage such a rapid productivity transformation. If the middle class fragments even more under AI, polarization is likely to intensify further. AI thus threatens not only economic disruption, but an accelerated replay of the same political failure that followed earlier productivity transitions.

V. Conclusion

Today, just 33% of voters believe the country is capable of solving its political problems, with polarization and the state of democracy cited as more urgent concerns than immigration, inflation, or crime.9 This pessimism reflects a widespread belief that both the political and the economic system are failing, and there is little consensus on how to move forward.

At home, the "strong" are those insulated from transitions — owners of capital, dominant firms, and economic elites able to convert technological change into durable, often generational, advantage. The "weak" are not just the poor, but a fragmented middle class repeatedly asked to absorb the costs of structural change without corresponding protection, compensation, or political voice.

Without confronting the politics of productivity and distribution directly, polarization is not a temporary aberration but a stable outcome. The strong continue to do what they can, and the weak suffer even more.
